BPC-157 Dosage Guide: How to Calculate, Reconstitute & Use a 5mg Vial Correctly

The most common mistakes with BPC-157 aren't about the peptide itself — they're about reconstitution math, syringe selection, and storage. This step-by-step guide gives you the exact numbers and technique for a 5mg vial, whether you're injecting subcutaneously or using the oral protocol for gut healing.

R
Russel B.
📅 May 2026 ⏱ 8 min read

What You'll Need

BPC-157 (Body Protective Compound-157) is a synthetic 15-amino acid peptide derived from a protein found in gastric juice. It is one of the most studied research peptides in the musculoskeletal and gastroenterology research space, with extensive rodent data on tendon repair, gut healing, angiogenesis, and neuroprotection. Before beginning any protocol, assembling the correct materials is essential — shortcuts here create unnecessary risk and inaccuracy.

For subcutaneous injection, you will need:

  • BPC-157 lyophilized powder in a sealed vial (5mg is the most common vial size)
  • Bacteriostatic water (BAC water) — this is sterile water containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol, which acts as a preservative and extends the stability of reconstituted peptides significantly compared to plain sterile water
  • U100 insulin syringes (1 mL capacity, 29–31 gauge needle) — these are calibrated in International Units (IU) on one side and milliliters on the other, making dose measurement precise
  • Alcohol swabs for sterilizing vial tops and injection sites
  • A clean, flat work surface

For the oral protocol (gut healing use), you will need BPC-157 powder, regular sterile water (not required to be BAC water since you will not be injecting it), and a clean oral syringe or cup. Oral BPC-157 does not require the same sterility precautions as injectable preparation, but cleanliness remains important.

Why BAC water and not sterile water for injections? Sterile water without a preservative is a single-use medium — once the vial is punctured and peptide is dissolved, microbial contamination can occur within days. BAC water's benzyl alcohol content inhibits bacterial growth, extending the usability of your reconstituted peptide to approximately 30 days when refrigerated. This is the standard recommendation across the research community and is important for both safety and economics.

Understanding Peptide Concentration

This is where most beginners go wrong. Peptide vials contain a dry (lyophilized) powder — BPC-157 is sold by mass (milligrams), not by volume. Concentration is determined entirely by how much liquid you add during reconstitution. The more liquid you add, the lower the concentration per milliliter, and the larger the volume you will need to draw for each dose.

The universal principle: Concentration (mcg/mL) = Total peptide (mcg) ÷ Total liquid added (mL).

For a 5mg vial, you have 5,000 mcg of BPC-157 to work with. The amount of BAC water you add determines your working concentration. Most practitioners use 1–2 mL of BAC water for a 5mg vial, producing concentrations that map conveniently to U100 syringe markings.

Step-by-Step Reconstitution

Follow these steps precisely to ensure sterility and peptide integrity:

  1. Prepare your workspace. Wash your hands thoroughly. Wipe your work surface with an alcohol swab. Gather all materials before starting.
  2. Wipe both vial tops — the BPC-157 vial and the BAC water vial — with fresh alcohol swabs. Let them air dry for 10–15 seconds.
  3. Draw BAC water. Using an insulin syringe, draw the desired volume of BAC water from its vial. For a 5mg BPC-157 vial, the most common choice is 2 mL (see concentration table in callout below). Drawing 2 mL may require two separate syringe fills with a 1 mL insulin syringe if you don't have a larger syringe available.
  4. Inject BAC water into the peptide vial slowly. Insert the needle through the rubber stopper at an angle and direct the BAC water stream gently down the inside wall of the vial — do not shoot it directly onto the lyophilized powder, as this can degrade the peptide through mechanical disruption.
  5. Do not shake the vial. Gently swirl or roll the vial between your palms until the powder is fully dissolved. The solution should be clear and colorless. If it appears cloudy or has visible particulate matter, do not use it.
  6. Label your vial with the date of reconstitution, the total peptide amount, the volume of BAC water added, and the resulting concentration. Store in the refrigerator immediately.

Calculating Your Dose

Using the 2 mL reconstitution (2,500 mcg/mL) as the working example:

The most commonly referenced research dose for BPC-157 is 250–500 mcg per injection, once or twice daily. At 2,500 mcg/mL concentration:

  • 250 mcg dose = 0.10 mL = 10 IU on a U100 syringe
  • 500 mcg dose = 0.20 mL = 20 IU on a U100 syringe

A U100 insulin syringe is calibrated with 100 units per mL. Each small graduation mark represents 2 IU (0.02 mL). This makes it straightforward to measure doses accurately once you know your concentration.

With a 5mg vial reconstituted in 2 mL (yielding 2,500 mcg/mL), your vial contains enough for:

  • 20 doses at 250 mcg
  • 10 doses at 500 mcg

At 250 mcg twice daily, a 5mg vial lasts 10 days — comfortably within the 30-day refrigerated stability window. Planning your supply accordingly prevents waste from expiration.

Storage: Lyophilized (unreconstituted) BPC-157 can be stored at room temperature for several months or frozen for longer-term storage. Once reconstituted with BAC water, the peptide solution should be refrigerated immediately and used within 28–30 days. Do not freeze reconstituted peptide — repeated freeze-thaw cycles degrade the peptide and reduce potency. Keep out of direct light; amber vials or foil wrapping protect the solution from UV degradation during storage.

Subcutaneous vs Oral BPC-157

BPC-157 has the unusual property — for a peptide — of showing biological activity via oral administration in rodent models. This has been demonstrated repeatedly in gastric ulcer, inflammatory bowel disease, and gut motility studies where the peptide was given in drinking water. The mechanism likely involves local action on GI tract receptors and tissues rather than systemic absorption, which explains why it doesn't need to survive full GI transit to show efficacy in gut-specific endpoints.

Subcutaneous injection is the route of choice for systemic effects — joint repair, tendon healing, neuroprotection, and anti-inflammatory effects throughout the body. The peptide is absorbed rapidly from subcutaneous tissue into the bloodstream and distributes systemically. For musculoskeletal injuries, injection near (but not directly into) the injury site is sometimes preferred, as higher local concentrations may enhance localized effects, though systemic injection at a distance also shows efficacy in animal models due to systemic distribution.

Injection technique for subcutaneous BPC-157: pinch a small fold of skin on the abdomen or outer thigh, insert the needle at a 45-degree angle, and inject slowly. Aspirating (pulling back the plunger before injecting) to check for blood is standard practice — if blood appears in the syringe, withdraw, discard, and use a fresh syringe. Do not inject into the same site repeatedly — rotate sites to prevent tissue irritation and subcutaneous bruising.

Oral BPC-157 is the preferred approach for gut-specific applications: leaky gut, gastric ulcers, IBS, Crohn's disease-related inflammation, or general gut healing protocols. For oral use, the peptide can be reconstituted in regular sterile water rather than BAC water (since you're not injecting it and the benzyl alcohol preservative, while generally safe orally at small amounts, is unnecessary). The solution is simply swallowed. Typical oral dosing mirrors injection dosing — 250–500 mcg once or twice daily — and some practitioners prefer taking oral BPC-157 on an empty stomach for maximum GI tract contact time.

The practical advantage of the oral route for gut healing is that it dramatically simplifies the protocol: no needles, no sterility management, no injection technique to master. Oral BPC-157 can be mixed into a small amount of water and consumed directly. The trade-off is that systemic bioavailability is lower, so oral BPC-157 is a poor choice for joint, tendon, or systemic anti-inflammatory applications where subcutaneous injection is significantly more effective.
